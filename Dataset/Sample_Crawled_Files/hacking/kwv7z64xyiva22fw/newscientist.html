Smart tool could spot dodgy domains and block botnets

Technology 8 February 2011

By Jacob Aron

Watch out spammers, phishers and botnets: a new tool designed to hunt down malicious web domains could illuminate your shady corners of the internet.

Internet service providers (ISPs) currently have no way of clearly and automatically identifying dodgy domains. “In general ISPs don’t know what’s going on in their network,” says computer security researcher Leyla Bilge at the International Secure Systems Lab near Antibes, France.

To combat the problem, Bilge and colleagues have developed Exposure, a tool which identifies malicious domains by monitoring traffic in a key part of internet infrastructure known as the domain name system (DNS).

DNS translates a web domain address that make sense to humans â such as www.newscientist.com â into a numerical internet protocol (IP) address like 194.201.25.21, which identifies the actual server that stores the site you want to connect to. One domain can point to multiple IP addresses, and domains can also move to different IP addresses, but the easy-to-remember domain name means that people can always access the correct server for the website they want to see.

Kingpin domain

This adaptability also helps fly-by-night online criminals keep control of their botnet empires, however. The infected computers within a botnet need to contact command-and-control servers for their instructions, but the botmaster must constantly move these command systems to different IP addresses to avoid being caught. DNS lets them use a fixed domain name while moving around without having to alter the code on infected machines.

Exposure analyses DNS traffic and looks for clues to malicious behaviour, such as domains that suddenly appear before disappearing shortly after attacks, or domain names containing lots of numbers and few meaningful words â domains used to control botnets are often automatically generated.

The system is trained on data from known malicious domains, learning that certain patterns of DNS traffic indicate particular kinds of internet crime. Working with a French ISP, Bilge was able to identify over 3000 previously unknown malicious domains. She is also running Exposure in real time on the web and reporting the top 50 malicious domains each day.

It’s easy to shut down or block domains once they’re identified, but tracking down the perpetrators behind them is more difficult. Malicious domains are often registered in countries like China or Russia that aren’t always cooperative in handing people over. “It’s not the final solution for botnets â you’re always fighting against them,” says Bilge.

Keeping up with crime

“I think it’s quite a valuable addition to the methods that we already have,” says Marco Cova, a computer scientist at the University of Birmingham, UK. Exposure’s learning ability would also be useful in continuously fighting against malicious domains, he says. “Once you describe a system someone can devise methods to bypass it,” he explains. “With a system like this it wouldn’t be hard to add a new feature to monitor.”

The International Secure Systems Lab team, which is based across five laboratories in France, Austria, the US and Germany, will present their work at the Network and Distributed System Security Symposium in San Diego, California today.
